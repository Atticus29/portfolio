Life Story

Like so many others fortunate enough to witness the birth of the internet in their youth, my first serious forays into programming (html and C++) were attempts to fit in with a peer group of teenage website makers and computer geeks in the late ninties. After cordially parting ways with both the peer group and programming for the allure and promise of college, I began what would become a lifelong love affair with ecosystems and critters (particularly ones that interact with one another in interesting ways) and selected Ecology and Evolutionary Biology (EEB) as my college major. This fascination with critters led to enrollment in a graduate program in Genetics, wherein I studied fire ant populations and inbreeding for six years. I left the program with a nice, crisp PhD and a refreshed enthusiasm for programming (forcing myself to learn some python and R are likely the only reasons that I was able to graduate) in 2013.

I then spent a sixth-month stint in Costa Rica running around studying moth populations near Monteverde, and then headed to Vancouver, WA, where I taught biology at Washington State Univeristy Vancouver for two years. Satisfied with and fulfilled by teaching as a career, but underwhelmed by the financial prospects of a long-term adjunct instructor, I applied for a position at Oregon Health and Science University (OHSU) as a computational biologist, where I was able to learn an incredible amount of biology and programming (command-line, job-scheduling, git, and a lot of R) before crumpling under the constant pressure and deciding to regroup and re-tool at Epicodus, where I am today.

I continue to love learning new things about programming and solving problems with code, and I continue to love ecosystems and critters (although bees have my attention more these days than ants do) and teaching. I hope some day to use the formermost to solve important problems or questions in the latter two. I'm eager to see where the adventures in coding take me in the meantime.
